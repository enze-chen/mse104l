Vegard‚Äôs law¬∂

Back to top

Recall that in the pre-lab you learned about Vegard‚Äôs law (which is pretty remarkable!) that says:

\[ a_{\text{A-B}} = a_{\text{A}} x_{\text{A}} + a_{\text{B}} x_{\text{B}} \]

where \(a_X\) and \(x_X\) are the lattice constant and concentration, respectively, of species \(X\). For the binary case where this applies, note \(x_{\text{B}} = 1 - x_{\text{A}}\).

Anyways, you‚Äôll do the Nelson-Riley calculation above and get a few values of \(a\) corresponding to different concentrations \(x\). You then have to plot this and comment on whether Vegard‚Äôs law is obeyed. There are several ways to answer this question, and we encourage you to be creative. Here are just two suggestions, one based on something you‚Äôve seen, and another based on something that you have probably heard before and we‚Äôll teach you how to calculate it.

  1. Like with Nelson-Riley plots, you can compute a line of best fit, plot that line, and look at the intercepts at \(x=0\) and \(x=1\). Do they agree with the experimental lattice constants of the pure metals? Can you quantify the amount of error?

  2. You can compute a linear correlation coefficient (also known as Pearson‚Äôs \(r\)) between your values of \(a\) and \(x\). Pearson‚Äôs \(r\) can be expressed mathematically as:

\[ r_{X, Y} = \frac{\text{covariance}(X, Y)}{\sigma(X) \sigma(Y)} \]

where \(\sigma(\cdot)\) is the standard deviation, and values of \(r\) range from \([-1, +1]\) as follows:
